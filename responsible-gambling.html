3. Recognizing a Potential Problem

Responsible gambling means being aware of your habits and recognizing when they might be changing. If you are concerned about your gambling, please consider the following questions honestly:

  • Do you spend more money or time gambling than you can comfortably afford?
  • Have you ever hidden the extent of your gambling from friends or family?
  • Are you chasing your losses by gambling more?
  • Has gambling negatively impacted your personal relationships, work, or studies?
  • Do you gamble as a way to escape from stress, boredom, or other problems?
  • Have you ever felt a sense of guilt or regret after gambling?
  • Do you find it difficult to stop or control your gambling, even when you want to?

If you answered "yes" to one or more of these questions, it may be a sign that you could benefit from seeking support. We urge you to speak to someone and to make immediate use of our self-exclusion tool.

Acknowledging that you may need help is a sign of strength. Several independent, non-profit organizations are dedicated to providing free, confidential, and professional support to anyone affected by problem gambling. We highly recommend contacting them:

  • Gamblers Anonymous: A global fellowship providing peer support for individuals with a gambling problem. Local meetings can be found across Canada.
    Website: www.gamblersanonymous.org
  • GamCare: While UK-based, it offers an excellent online forum and resources that are valuable for anyone, anywhere.
    Website: www.gamcare.org.uk
  • ConnexOntario: Provides free and confidential health services information for people in Ontario experiencing problems with alcohol and drugs, mental illness, or gambling.
    Website: www.connexontario.ca
  • Responsible Gambling Council (RGC): A leading Canadian non-profit organization dedicated to problem gambling prevention.
    Website: www.responsiblegambling.org
